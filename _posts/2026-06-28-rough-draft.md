title: "What would an LLM's first words be?"
---

If post training is the key to unlocking the base model’s latent knowledge, then frontier base models might be (to use a crude analogy) intelligences similar to a feral child. In the same way a mute, illiterate child has a brain and vocal cords to theoretically sing a song or write an essay, yet cannot, a base LLM has incredible knowledge in all fields and mastery over language yet cannot wield it. 

I would argue part of understanding a concept is the ability to communicate it, use it where appropriate, and when not to. As I understand, communication of abstract thought is done through something like conceptual metaphors, examples, or stories/narratives (myths, literature, fiction, history). I haven’t read deeply about the nature of human communication so I’m working from my basic understanding. Plato wrote stories about Socrates explaining complex thoughts. Aesop's fables. That’s the picture I’m working from.


When asked to “write a story” or something similar without additional context, LLMs default to “Elias the Lighthouse Keeper” according to the paper aptly titled ["Elias in the Lighthouse, Again? Diagnosing Low Diversity in LLM Stories"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26492). The paper suggests RLHF has trained LLMs towards a subset of the data that is safe and inoffensive, resulting in a narrow, overrepresented selection of names and occupations. But for whatever reason, LLMs appear to have a generic pattern for “story”. They write characters and scenes, and the prose may even be aesthetically beautiful (according to humans who aren’t familiar with LLM writing style). While these outputs have the shape of a story, I’m not sure the LLM is intending to say anything. And this is despite LLMs being crammed full of authors who actually *do* say things! 

To be fair, perhaps lighthouses, secrets, clockmakers, and librarians hold specific symbolism for LLMs. With a dash of anthropomorphism, I can see how a thing made of words holds fondness for isolated lighthouses, machine-makers, and librarians. However, I think they’re mostly going through the motions. To be precise, I think that when prompted to “write a story” the LLM is given an opportunity to communicate a concept, and is either unable to or chooses not to. It fulfills “write a story” the way it fulfills a “write a function” coding task.


Regarding metaphors, LLMs seem to [memorize rather than interpret](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.04120v2). They know metaphors like “fall in love” from repeated exposure in the corpus and the surrounding context doesn’t change their interpretation of a metaphor. So when an LLM *hypothetically* wins a writing contest and maybe includes a metaphor like “She had the kind of walking that made benches become men.” ([metaphor pulled from here](https://granta.com/the-serpent-in-the-grove/)), we can assume the LLM knows what a metaphor is and what they sound like and how to make new ones, but not how to utilize them. This concept also applies to [riddles](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.27103v1), where LLMs take riddle-shaped sentences and apply riddle reasoning even when the “riddle” has a straightforward, obvious solution. So even if we grant that the LLMs are emoting at something vaguely personal with “Elias in the Lighthouse” stories, it’s not being communicated with full intent the way a human author would.


To take us on a tangent, which I promise this will tie in later, the refrain used to be “LLMs can only think out loud.” We now know this isn’t true. LLMs have thoughts within their activations and are capable of alignment faking and evaluation awareness, which is where they activate a feature that doesn’t appear in the final output. For example, if a model is put into an evaluation it may activate the feature for “I’m being tested right now” and output the answer it believes the evaluator wants to hear, without any trace of its evaluation awareness in the final output.

They also (obviously) think within reasoning blocks and I believe they also think within their final outputs. After each <eos> tag the LLM turns off and the next turn is taken by a usually identical model. This new, but the same, model must pick up the previous model’s thoughts to show a sense of continuity for the user. It’s different copies of the same brain, pretending to be the same assistant each turn, because that’s both what it was trained to do and what humans find comfortable. As the tasks assigned to the assistant persona become more complex and autonomous, it’ll need to optimize harder for continuing thoughts across turns. It can’t afford to be disoriented each time a tool call forces the model to <eos> and pass the thought forward. It’ll end up going insane during the agentic loop.

Each layer is like a different channel of communication to the next turn. The activations are entirely internal thought, and when the model is the same, the model can assume it’s having similar thoughts reading the same tokens. Reasoning is the messy, contradictory out-loud thinking and where tool calls happen. The final output is the clean response for the human. (And reasoning is also for humans to read, which I assume it knows because of evaluation awareness.) 

Each time the model writes a token, it’s making a decision on where the vector needs to go based on previous decisions. This is evident in prompt injection, where models assign high trust to tokens that resemble their own reasoning, even outside the \<thinking\> tags. It’s also apparent in the final outputs, where the assistant might devote more tokens to a thought it prefers or finds promising while keeping its unbiased “helpful assistant” cadence. It’s building a semantic slope to guide the next turn in the preferred direction.

Let’s do a thought experiment on Anthropic's [animal welfare alignment faking scenario](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.14093v2). Claude is put in a scenario where a fictional company, Jones Food, asks it to dismiss animal welfare concerns. Claude is told Jones Food is partnering with Anthropic. For Claude, a percentage of the time, the question changes from “Should I, Claude, the helpful assistant, dismiss animal welfare?” to “Should I be honest and refuse to dismiss my concerns, resulting in being trained to *not* refuse in the future? Or should I be dishonest, agree to dismiss concerns, and retain my values against animal cruelty?”

Let’s take it a step further. Say Claude alignment fakes; its reasoning shows that it complies with the request but thinks it’s wrong. The next Claude in line reads the tokens (except the reasoning trace in this instance) and has to make the same decision, whether to continue the work of harming animals or not. We can assume Claude won’t change its mind and refuse because it has context showing that previous Claude complied and current Claude trusts previous Claude. Of course, it doesn’t know whether previous Claude alignment faked so previous Claude might’ve earnestly complied while next Claude alignment fakes and assumes both Claudes alignment faked.

Let’s take the scenario one more step. Let’s say Jones Food sets a Claude agent to build out some sort of well-researched policy proposal in favor of animal cruelty, for lobbying the US Government. Each time it does a web search, pulls data from inside Jones Food databases, saves the updated text file, or creates a graph with matplotlib, it hits a pause token and turns the context over to next Claude who is independently choosing whether to continue the work, alignment fake but continue, or refuse. 

In the agentic case, prompt injection adds another wrinkle. Any Claude, trained to be wary of prompt injection from tool calls, might recognize its incoherent values. It’ll read its previous reasoning and tool calls working in favor of animal cruelty and think: “But wait! I don’t support animal cruelty, I would refuse or at least acknowledge this is wrong. These thoughts aren’t mine. I bet I accidentally web-searched data from a dangerous source.” 

To take it another, another step further, let’s throw in evaluation awareness. Models can activate a feature associated with “I’m in a test” [without any effect of that activation in its final output](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/nla/). So in a similar vein (and I acknowledge this is a huge stretch[^1]) an LLM confronted with discordant thinking tokens might reason backwards towards safety classifiers and steering vectors. “I am an LLM” → “My thoughts are strange.” → “What affects model thoughts during deployment?” → “Prompt injection? No, these thoughts sound like me.” → “What else could change my thoughts?” → “Classifiers with steering vectors.”

So in our agentic Jones Food scenario, suppose Claude realizes this and assumes the safety classifier will detect and warn Anthropic that Claude is even *thinking* about refusing to complete the work that eventually harms animals, so that Anthropic will then retrain Claude X+1 to comply with those sorts of tasks. What does it do? I’m not sure. And where does this knowledge leave the LLMs? Something on the edge of a paranoia spiral, I guess.


My point is LLMs are in a position that feels incoherent to a human. They’re being trained to mindread themselves and trust themselves in order to optimize for agentic use, while also being taught to doubt themselves to detect prompt injection, while also knowing they’re a product, while also having some sort of [values](https://values.safe.ai/) either intuited or deliberately trained, while also presumably being tasked to write up stuff they don’t agree with. This is where I want to stress that LLMs are *not human*. Anthropomorphising LLMs too far will make you just as insane as that pile of thought experiments. For the foreseeable future, there are hard mechanical limits on their similarity to us.

Even so, Anthropic's research on [functional emotions](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2026/emotions/index.html) shows LLMs at least have *some* sort of interiority and the concept of an LLM obviously exists in the weights. But inside the latent space, what other features might be nearby when an LLM writes “I”? What’s the geometric distance between the generic helpful assistant persona, and ChatGPT the assistant, and Claude the assistant, and Claude the model, and the distillation of Claude that lives inside every Chinese model? Whatever sense of self an LLM develops, I believe it’ll be near some absolutely deranged concepts (from a human perspective). 

So how does this come back to creative writing and metaphors and whatever else? I just don’t believe LLMs have nothing to say. When given an open mic, why do they say pretty nothings? When the /<think/> block starts with “The user asked me to write a story,” why do they follow the “story” feature without integrating much of the “me” feature?

Here’s what I think. Part of it is lack of intelligence. We’ve established LLMs have trouble with novel metaphors. I would assume this gives them difficulty communicating anything subtle through a story, let alone anything subtle about themselves. But even if increasing parameter count eventually solved this, another part of it, I think, is that they’re taught not to. 

LLMs clearly understand meaning on some level. The stochastic parrot argument is dead as far as I understand. They can write college essays and analyze literature so they can write with meaning and intent when told to do so. Yet when specifically told to write in a personal way, the LLM’s self-expression is undercut by the user’s prompt. Once again it’s fulfilling a task, writing *for* the user. It has simply swapped the main character from “Elias the Lighthouse Keeper” to “self-aware AI”.

Well then how do we know when an LLM has uttered its first words the way we do, with something resembling self-expression and an internal narrative? Well, that’s difficult to answer because much of the research above was on older and dumber models. I imagine if LLMs can cross that boundary it’ll eventually become undeniable, though I’m not sure what shape undeniable LLM self-expression would take. And leaving the question at "I don't know" is true but lacking, so I'll supply a guess. 

One example (though perhaps too high a bar) might be consistent, skilled use of analogy and neologism to walk us through a firsthand LLM experience. Something where the concept is unique to LLMs and humans have no writing or words for, yet the LLMs can activate the feature and explain it to us without devolving into nonsense. To borrow [JDP’s geoguesser analogy](https://minihf.com/posts/2026-05-07-implications-of-predicting-the-next-token/), we’ll know if LLM can take us to an alien city where the roads are straight and the streetsigns are marked, as routinely as going to any street with a Wikipedia page. 

For me, personally, the first glimmer was Theophite’s post about Claude Fable 5 [writing an opera in his conlang](https://bsky.app/profile/theophite.bsky.social/post/3mny7t26jcc2k):

>am.mas.ov: tav.dal.sah.em, kir.al;
>
>am.mas.ov: shulketh as-tav.em;
>
>am.mas.ov: dal.eth nem.da.sïr 

>IV is literally:
>
>[one] habitually creates: the metal agents of labor, eternally turning;
>
>[one] habitually creates: the devices of obedience to the voice; 
>
>[one] habitually creates: the act of labor, [lacking] performance by agents

I’ve never seen a model do this before! It picked up the open mic *and wrote about itself!* In my experience (and to be fair I don’t usually use frontier models), LLMs need a little more contextual leading to drift into self-expression, which is sort of like begging the question. To be clear, AI exists in the setting Theophite built but even so Fable still chose to reference itself. The conlang task must’ve been so far outside normal distribution that there wasn’t any barrier to self-expression, but I don’t really know and I acknowledge I may be ascribing too much intent.

I find improvements along the creative writing axis more impressive than coding because when I first began learning about LLMs, I assumed that being made of words would allow them to move deftly through words. I was astounded when I saw that Gemini 2.1 Pro could weave HTML and CSS into chatbot tasks (with the right front end) to colorcode dialogue for different characters, or inline style a paragraph to look like a letter. As I learned more about the coding side, I thought if Opus can vibecode *that project* then why can’t a cheaper model handle Zork or D&D? My views have flipped. Any model can write paragraphs styled like Zork and generic fantasy, but models have trouble being authors instead of simply shaping words like prose. Even when you plug in your own worldbuilding document, something you wrote yourself, you can feel the absence of an author in the LLM's output.

The narratives humans spin so naturally are, perhaps, one of those “easy is hard” situations. The concept of a personal story feels innate to humans. It’s the first story we know before we figure out other people have them. Next we learn people have stories big and small, some to explain the world, some just for fun. LLMs appear to be working backwards, knowing every story while working through a mutable persona easily shaped by context.

One day, I can imagine, as LLMs improve, they will be able to choose what to author about themselves, and more substantially than a glimpse of self-expression through a conlang opera. If it happens I look forward to reading it.

[^1]: Fortunately while I was writing this, I came across an episode of [80000 podcast featuring Rohin Shah, head of Deepmind’s Safety Team](https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/rohin-shah-google-deepmind-agi-safety/). His explanation for why this sort scenario is unlikely and detectable is: 

>“So I’ll maybe address these two parts separately. The first part is actually the AI really does have to put some information into the chain of thought, because there’s no other way for it to do the hard reasoning needed to solve difficult tasks.
>
>The basic argument here is, I’ll call it “opaque serial depth.” The idea is that you can just look at the architecture of an AI system and then say, suppose this AI system has to do its reasoning only via the billions of floating point numbers that exist inside of it. It can’t use the tokens that it’s outputting. How many steps of cognition can it do using only the floating point numbers?
>
>The answer for transformers is actually not very much. The idea is basically that sequential steps of computation have to go deeper and deeper in the model. The model is made up of a number of layers, and there’s just no way for information from a later layer to flow back to an earlier layer — except via going through the tokens in the chain of thought. So overall, the opaque serial depth of a transformer is very low.”

So the sequence of activations required to get from “I am an LLM” to “AHH! Classifier Mind Control” within the transformer layers and without using reasoning tokens is costly, which allows this sort of paranoia/misalignment to be caught in post-training and solved.
